# 📚 Repertoire & Études Guide — Your Learning Ladder

*Use this as a roadmap — you don't need to play everything, but follow the level order.*

## 2. Repertoire by Level

### Level 1–2: First Pieces (Months 1–6)

```
  Folk songs and simple melodies:
  ● Hot Cross Buns
  ● Mary Had a Little Lamb
  ● Twinkle Twinkle Little Star (Suzuki #1)
  ● Lightly Row
  ● Go Tell Aunt Rhody
  ● Ode to Joy (Beethoven)

  Suzuki Book 1 pieces:
  ● Long, Long Ago
  ● Allegretto
  ● Allegro
  ● Minuet No. 1 (Bach)
  ● Minuet No. 2 (Bach)
  ● Gavotte (Gossec)

  Focus: clean tone, rhythm, basic finger patterns
```

### Level 3: Upper Beginner (Months 6–12)

```
  Suzuki Book 2:
  ● Musette (Bach)
  ● Hunters' Chorus (Weber)
  ● Two Grenadiers (Schumann)
  ● Witches' Dance (Paganini — simplified)

  Other pieces:
  ● Simple arrangements of classical melodies
  ● Easy duets with teacher or another student

  Focus: dynamics, slurs, 4th finger, string crossings
```

### Level 4: Intermediate (Year 1–2)

```
  Suzuki Book 3–4:
  ● Gavotte (Martini)
  ● Minuet in G (Bach)
  ● Gavotte in G Minor (Bach)
  ● Humoresque (Dvořák)
  ● Seitz: Student Concertos (No. 2, No. 5)

  Concertinos:
  ● Küchler: Concertino in D Major, Op. 15
  ● Rieding: Concerto in B Minor, Op. 35
  ● Rieding: Concertino in G Major, Op. 24

  Focus: 3rd position, shifting, vibrato, musical expression
```

### Level 5: Advanced Intermediate (Year 2–3)

```
  Concertos:
  ● Vivaldi: Concerto in A Minor, Op. 3 No. 6
  ● Vivaldi: Concerto in G Minor, Op. 12 No. 1
  ● Accolay: Concerto No. 1 in A Minor
  ● Bach: Concerto in A Minor, BWV 1041
  ● Bach: Concerto in E Major, BWV 1042

  Solo Bach (selected movements):
  ● Partita No. 3: Preludio, Gavotte en Rondeau
  ● Partita No. 1: Sarabande, Tempo di Bourrée

  Sonatas:
  ● Handel: Violin Sonatas
  ● Corelli: Sonatas Op. 5

  Show pieces:
  ● Kreisler: Liebesleid, Liebesfreud
  ● Massenet: Méditation from Thaïs

  Focus: all positions (1-7), advanced bowing, harmonics, musicality
```

### Level 6: Advanced (Year 3–5)

```
  Major Concertos:
  ● Mozart: Concerto No. 3 in G Major, K. 216
  ● Mozart: Concerto No. 4 in D Major, K. 218
  ● Mozart: Concerto No. 5 in A Major, K. 219 "Turkish"
  ● Haydn: Concerto No. 1 in C Major
  ● Kabalevsky: Concerto in C Major, Op. 48
  ● Bruch: Concerto No. 1 in G Minor (start working on it)

  Solo Bach (complete works):
  ● Sonatas and Partitas for Solo Violin, BWV 1001-1006
  ● These are considered the "Old Testament" of violin

  Sonatas:
  ● Beethoven: Sonata No. 5 "Spring"
  ● Mozart: Sonata in E Minor, K. 304
  ● Franck: Sonata in A Major (start)

  Virtuoso Pieces:
  ● Kreisler: Praeludium & Allegro
  ● Wieniawski: Légende, Op. 17
  ● Sarasate: Zigeunerweisen (start)
  ● Monti: Csárdás

  Focus: full musical and technical command, interpretation, performance
```

### Level 7: Professional / Pre-Conservatory (Year 5+)


## 4. Chamber Music — Playing with Others

```
  Chamber music is a vital part of a violinist's education.

  Start with:
  ● Duets — play with your teacher or another student
  ● Easy trios (2 violins + piano/cello)
  ● Suzuki ensemble arrangements

  Intermediate:
  ● Haydn: String Quartets (easier ones: Op. 1, Op. 2)
  ● Mozart: Eine kleine Nachtmusik (violin 1 or 2)
  ● Handel: Trio Sonatas

  Advanced:
  ● Beethoven: String Quartets (Op. 18 set)
  ● Mozart: String Quartets
  ● Schubert: "Death and the Maiden" Quartet
  ● Dvořák: "American" Quartet
  ● Brahms: Piano Trios, Quartets, Quintets
  ● Mendelssohn: Octet

  Skills developed by chamber music:
  - Listening to others
  - Blending your sound
  - Following a different voice
  - Rhythmic precision without a conductor
  - Musical communication
```

## 5. How to Choose Repertoire

```
  ASK YOURSELF:

  1. Can I play the hardest passage at 60% tempo after a few tries?
     YES → Good level for you
     NO  → Too hard right now, come back later

  2. Does the piece teach me something new?
     New key / new technique / new style → Great choice
     Everything is familiar → Too easy (unless for performance)

  3. Do I ENJOY it?
     You practice better when you love the music.
     Balance "what I should learn" with "what I love to play."

  4. Am I studying a variety of styles?
     Baroque (Bach, Vivaldi, Handel)
     Classical (Mozart, Haydn, Beethoven)
     Romantic (Bruch, Mendelssohn, Tchaikovsky)
     20th Century (Bartók, Prokofiev, Shostakovich)
     → Each era teaches different skills.

  5. Am I balancing concertos with sonatas and solo works?
     Concertos = virtuosity and projection
     Sonatas = collaboration and musical depth
     Solo Bach = self-reliance and polyphonic thinking
```
